# UTT 59 km Readiness — Evidence Review of the Recalibrated Plan

**Date:** 2026-06-27 · **Goal:** finish strong & healthy · **Race:** Ultra Trail Tarahumara 59 km / 2400 m vert, Copper Canyon, Oct 2 2026
**Method:** deep-research harness — 24 sources, 92 claims, 25 adversarially verified (22 confirmed / 3 killed), 8 synthesized findings.

## Verdict

The plan (peak 48 km/wk, 32 km longest run, 1900 m vert at wk 27, 3-wk taper, 3:1 build/recovery) gets you to a **reasonable but not fully sufficient** state to finish *strong and healthy*. Structure (periodization + taper) is best-practice aligned. Three gaps cost durability and are all closeable in the time left.

## What the plan already gets right (validated)

- **Periodization** — 10–15%/wk for 2–3 wks then a recovery week is the recommended ramp; your ~10–13% + 3:1 matches it. *(medium — iRunFar; caveat: single long-run jumps predict injury better than weekly total)*
- **3-week taper** — cut volume 41–60%, **hold intensity**; disciplined ~3-wk tapers gave best endurance outcomes. *(high — PLOS One 2023 meta-analysis; Smyth & Lawlor 2021, n=158k)*

## The 3 gaps to close (prioritized)

### 1. Downhill / eccentric quad conditioning — highest leverage *(high confidence)*
A single downhill bout: **CK ~613% of baseline at 24h, force −16%, explosive RFD −37%**, recovering only by day 3–4. Habitual downhill-trained runners **retain** quad strength (+4%) vs untrained (−9%). Copper Canyon = sustained technical descents → untrained quads collapse late-race.
→ **Add dedicated downhill-running intervals** to build eccentric tolerance (repeated-bout protection). Sources: MDPI Sports 14(1):12; PMC11129977; coppercanyons.com.

### 2. Vert ceiling + single long run *(medium)*
Peak weekly vert **1900 m < race 2400 m**, and only one 32 km long run. You could reach race day never having accumulated race-equivalent climb/descent in a session.
→ **Nudge peak vert toward/over 2400 m**, and add **back-to-back long runs** (2nd day −25–50%, every 2–3 wks) for time-on-feet on tired legs. *(Back-to-back guidance 3-0; iRunFar. Counterpoint: some elites avoid them — recommended, not mandatory.)*

### 3. Autoregulate the ramp + protect sleep *(high)*
**Sleeping <7h raised injury risk +51%; >7h reduced it −37%** (Johnston 2020). Your last 3 weeks show elevated resting HR (~+10%), HRV dipping to ~40, readiness ~49, morning Body Battery ~40, some 3.7h nights — mild under-recovery *despite low load*.
→ **Gate each week's volume increase on a 7-day HRV/readiness trend**; repeat/reduce a week when resting HR is up and HRV/readiness suppressed. Treat **>7h sleep as a non-negotiable**.

## Lock in fueling NOW *(high)*
Events >2h: **60–90 g carb/h** (the right target for a 6–7h trail ultra). Above ~60 g/h use a **2:1 glucose:fructose** blend (dual transporters ↑ oxidation ~50%). **Gut-train 6–10 weeks out** — 1 day/wk, +10 g/h every 1–2 wks, practicing exact race products on long/back-to-back runs. 120 g/h is unnecessary here. *(Jeukendrup/ACSM/ISSN via Precision Hydration, TrainingPeaks, TheFeed.)* **The 6–10 wk window means start now.**

## Strength *(low confidence)*
No strength-frequency claim survived verification. 2×/wk is a reasonable floor; the higher-value refinement is making it **eccentric / lower-limb / descent-specific** (heavy squats, step-downs, downhill repeats) rather than the session count.

## Killed claims (rigor)
- ✗ "Copper Canyon terrain is consistently technical throughout" — it's **mixed** runnable + technical, not uniformly brutal (0-3).
- ✗ "60–70 mi/wk is a hard volume ceiling for most ultrarunners" (0-3).
- ✗ A specific downhill-trained CK-blunting figure (0-3) — though the broader eccentric-damage finding held.

## Honest caveats
Flagship downhill-damage numbers come from **one small study** (n=10); squat-retention from an exploratory observational study (n=36) that couldn't isolate downhill vs strength effects. Sleep-injury point estimates are from one study (a 2025 meta-analysis found a weaker OR 1.34 — direction robust, magnitude likely overstated). Taper evidence is from road/lower-altitude populations using speed outcomes. Fueling/periodization lean on reputable **secondary** sources citing primary research. The recommendations close margins; they don't correct fundamental errors.
